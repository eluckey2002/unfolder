# Interview transcript — Priya
Date: 2026-05-16 (synthetic)
Interviewer: UX researcher (neutral, evidence-seeking)
Interviewee: Priya, 27, Chicago marketing coordinator and weekend papercraft hobbyist (~15 builds in two years)

## Section 1 — Warmup

**Interviewer:** Thanks for hopping on. To start, tell me a little about yourself — what you do, where you live, the kind of stuff you make.

**Priya:** Sure. I'm Priya, I'm 27, I live in a one-bedroom in Chicago, and I work as a marketing coordinator. The papercraft thing is purely a hobby — it lives on my dining table on weekends. I mostly make low-poly animals and figurines. There's a fox on my bookshelf, a deer, a couple of crystals, a pug I made for a friend's birthday, and a low-poly Pokémon I'm a little embarrassed about but also kind of proud of. So, maybe fifteen builds in two years?

**Interviewer:** How did you get into it?

**Priya:** Honestly, a YouTube video. It was one of those viral things — somebody time-lapsing a low-poly fox build, very satisfying, instrumental music, glue stick everywhere. I watched it twice and ordered cardstock that night. That was almost exactly two years ago.

**Interviewer:** And how often do you make something now?

**Priya:** It's binge-y. I'll do nothing for a month and then spend two weekends in a row on one model. A build is, like, twelve to twenty hours of actual cutting and folding, so it's not casual. When the mood strikes, basically.

## Section 2 — Most recent project, end to end

**Interviewer:** Tell me about the last papercraft thing you actually built.

**Priya:** The last one I finished was the pug, for my friend Megan's birthday in April. Low-poly pug, sitting pose, about the size of a softball. Cream and tan paper.

**Interviewer:** Walk me through it — from "I want to make this" to "it's sitting on the shelf."

**Priya:** Okay. So I knew Megan's birthday was coming and she has a pug, so I just Googled "papercraft pug PDF free." That's literally how I search every time. I found a couple of options on this site — I don't even remember the name, one of those papercraft template aggregators — and one of them was a finished PDF with the pages already laid out and numbered. That's the gold standard for me. Downloaded it, printed it on 110-pound cardstock — I'll come back to the cardstock thing — and then it was just cut, score, fold, glue. Took maybe fourteen hours over a weekend and a Tuesday night.

**Interviewer:** And if you hadn't found a finished PDF? What's the fallback?

**Priya:** [laughs] Honestly? I pick a different model. I know that sounds bad. But like — I'll give you a concrete example. Three weeks ago I was browsing Thingiverse, which I normally don't do for papercraft because it's a 3D printing site, but I'd seen someone on r/papercraft mention you can sometimes use STLs. And I found this gorgeous fox — way more detailed than the one on my shelf, more angular, almost geometric-jewel-looking. I downloaded the STL. And then I just… stared at it. Because I have no way to turn an STL into a PDF. So I closed the tab. That fox is still sitting in my downloads folder.

**Interviewer:** What part of the pug build took longest?

**Priya:** Cutting. It's always cutting. There were like sixty pieces and each one has these tiny tabs you have to cut around. My hand cramps. Folding goes fast once everything's scored. Gluing is the fun part actually — that's where it starts to look like a thing.

**Interviewer:** What frustrated you most?

**Priya:** On the pug specifically? Honestly not much, because the PDF was good. The frustration is always *before* the build, when I'm hunting for a usable file. When I want a model and there's only an STL, that's the frustrating part. The build itself is meditative.

**Interviewer:** What was fun?

**Priya:** Watching the face come together. With low-poly animals the face is always the last thing and it's like — suddenly there's a pug looking at you. That moment is why I do this.

## Section 3 — Tools and workflow

**Interviewer:** What software, if any, do you use as part of papercraft?

**Priya:** Almost none. That's kind of my whole deal. My "workflow" is: Google, download PDF, print, build. The only software in the loop is whatever opens a PDF.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever tried unfolding software?

**Priya:** I've tried. Three times, I think, all unsuccessfully. The first time I downloaded Pepakura's free viewer because somebody on Reddit said it was the standard. The viewer opens files but won't let you export anything, which I didn't realize until I'd installed it and poked at it for an hour. Then I did the paid Pepakura free trial. I opened it and the UI looked like — this is going to sound mean — it looked like accounting software from like 2003. All these little toolbars and dropdowns and a gray menu bar. I couldn't even figure out how to load my STL. I clicked around for forty minutes and quit.

**Interviewer:** And the third time?

**Priya:** Blender. I watched three "Export Paper Model" tutorials on YouTube. They all start with "first, install Blender" and then they're suddenly in this purple interface with a million panels. I downloaded the Blender installer, opened it, and there's a whole — I think it asked me about a system path or something. I closed it. I never even got Blender to launch. That was probably a year ago and I haven't tried since.

**Interviewer:** Was there a tool you wanted to use but bounced off?

**Priya:** All three of those. Pepakura especially, because I know it *would* work if I learned it. It's not that I can't learn software — I use Figma at work, I'm not afraid of tools. But I'm not going to spend a weekend learning Pepakura when I could spend that weekend actually folding a model. The math isn't there for me.

**Interviewer:** If you had to draw your workflow on a whiteboard, what would the boxes be?

**Priya:** [thinks] Box one: Google for a PDF. Box two: if PDF exists, download. Box three: print on cardstock. Box four: cut, score, fold, glue. Box five: post the finished thing to Instagram. There's no software box. If box one fails — no PDF available — the whole thing terminates. I just pick a different animal.

## Section 4 — Pain probes

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time a model came out wrong. What happened?

**Priya:** Oh, the deer. The deer I made maybe nine months ago — that was when I was still figuring out paper weight. I used 65-pound paper because that's what I had, and the legs couldn't hold the body. It like, slowly slumped over the course of a day. By the next morning it was a deer lying down. I tried to glue popsicle sticks inside the legs but it never really stood right. I kept it but it's in a drawer.

**Interviewer:** Did that change anything in how you work?

**Priya:** Yeah, that's when I went down the rabbit hole on paper weight. I asked on r/papercraft and the consensus is 110-pound cardstock for most models. Took me probably $30 of wrong cardstock to land there — I bought 65-pound, 80-pound, this textured 100-pound that wouldn't fold cleanly. Now I just buy the same 110-pound bright white from the same place every time.

**Interviewer:** When you print a sheet and start cutting, are you ever surprised by what's on it?

**Priya:** With a good PDF, no. The good ones are numbered and grouped. Sometimes the numbering is weird — like piece 47 is on page 2 and piece 48 is on page 5 for no reason. But that's a minor thing. With the one time I almost got Pepakura working — on the trial — the export was confusing. The pieces were placed efficiently for paper-saving but they weren't grouped by, like, body part. So all the head pieces were scattered across three sheets. That made assembly harder.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever wished a piece were split differently than the tool gave you? Or merged?

**Priya:** Well, on the Pepakura trial — I should clarify, I did manage to get one export out before I gave up. It was a low-poly rabbit. And when it came out, two of the pieces overlapped on the page. Like, the tool literally drew them on top of each other. I knew enough to know that was bad. And I looked at it and thought, okay, one of these needs to be cut somewhere different. But I didn't know which cut was the "right" one. Like, is there a cut that preserves the rabbit and a cut that doesn't? I had no idea. I ended up just splitting the bigger piece in half at what looked like a natural seam and hoping. The rabbit came out okay-ish. One side of its face is a little flatter than the other. I think it's because of my fix.

**Interviewer:** How do you handle tabs and glue flaps?

**Priya:** Whatever the PDF gives me, I use. Most of them have the little trapezoid tabs on alternating edges. I don't think about them. I've never modified one.

**Interviewer:** After it's assembled, what does the result usually look like — clean, "good enough," or "I can see all the seams"?

**Priya:** Honestly, "good enough." From three feet away they look great. Up close you can see the seams and some glue squeeze-out and the occasional crooked edge. I'm okay with that. It's handmade. The fox on my shelf has one ear that's slightly bent and I've never fixed it.

## Section 5 — Edit and undo behaviors

**Interviewer:** Once a model is unfolded by software, do you ever change anything before printing?

**Priya:** I never have, because I've basically never *successfully* unfolded a model. The rabbit I mentioned — I edited it in the sense that I split a piece with scissors after printing. Not in the software. The software part was so painful I just printed what it gave me and worked around it on paper.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever undone something in a papercraft tool?

**Priya:** Not really, no. I haven't used the tools enough to need undo.

**Interviewer:** Do you save your in-progress unfolds, or start fresh?

**Priya:** N/A for me. I just download PDFs. I do save the PDFs though — I have a folder called "papercraft" with everything I've ever printed, in case I want to make another one or share with a friend.

## Section 6 — Assembly help

**Interviewer:** When you're assembling, how do you keep track of which piece goes where?

**Priya:** The numbers on the pieces, mostly. Every good PDF labels each face with a number and tells you which number glues to which number. I also keep my laptop open next to me with either the original photo of the finished model — like the reference image from the PDF — or a YouTube build video of the same model if there is one.

**Interviewer:** Do you ever lose your place?

**Priya:** All the time. Mostly when I take a break for a day. I'll come back and have a half-built pug and a pile of pieces and I'll be like, okay, what was I doing. Usually I just look at the reference photo and the next-highest unused number and work it out.

**Interviewer:** Do you use anything besides the printed sheet to assemble?

**Priya:** Yeah — reference photo always, sometimes a YouTube timelapse of the same model if I can find one, and once in a while I'll rotate the 3D model on the source page on my phone. That's pretty rare though because I usually don't have a 3D model, I just have the PDF.

## Section 7 — Magic wand

**Interviewer:** If a software fairy could give you any one feature for your next papercraft project, what would it be?

**Priya:** Drop the STL on a webpage, get a PDF, done. That is literally the feature. I have this Thingiverse fox in my downloads folder right now. I want to drag it onto a website, wait however long, and download a printable PDF with numbered pages and reference images. No install. No trial. No purple interface. Done.

**Interviewer:** What's the silliest, most over-the-top thing you'd want?

**Priya:** [laughs] Oh, can I be ridiculous? Okay — a magic wand version. The website also tells me how much cardstock I'll need, how long it'll take a person of my speed to build, and shows me an AR preview on my phone of the finished thing on my actual bookshelf so I can pick a size that fits. And then it ships me the cut pieces so I don't have to cut them. That's the dream.

**Interviewer:** If unfolding software disappeared entirely and the model just magically appeared as a perfect PDF, would you miss anything about the current process?

**Priya:** [pause] No. I really wouldn't. The folding and gluing is the part I love. The software is — it's a gate, not a feature. If I could skip the gate I'd skip it tomorrow.

## Section 8 — Cool-down

**Interviewer:** Anything I should have asked that I didn't?

**Priya:** Maybe… about cost? Like, I have never paid for unfolding software and I'm not sure I would. I'd pay something for a finished PDF — I've bought a few off Etsy, five or ten bucks each — but paying $50 or whatever Pepakura is, for software I'd use four times a year, doesn't compute. I'd rather pay per PDF.

**Interviewer:** Anything you want to make sure is in the notes?

**Priya:** Just — I want to be clear, I'm not technical-illiterate or whatever. I think when I say "I'm not technical" it sounds like I can't use tools, and that's not it. I can use tools. I just don't want to use a tool when I could be folding paper. The tool needs to disappear into the background. If it makes me think about itself, it's already lost me.

**Interviewer:** Thanks, Priya. This was really helpful.

**Priya:** Yeah, anytime. If somebody actually builds that drop-the-STL-get-a-PDF thing, please tell them I will use it the day it ships.
