# Interview transcript — Sarah
Date: 2026-05-16 (synthetic)
Interviewer: UX researcher (neutral, evidence-seeking)
Interviewee: Sarah, 38, suburban pharmacist and weekend craft parent of two

## Section 1 — Warmup

**Interviewer:** Tell me a bit about yourself — what you do, where you live, what kind of stuff you make.

**Sarah:** Sure. I'm a pharmacist, I work at a chain in town. We're in a suburb, kind of typical — house, yard, two kids, ages seven and ten. The papercraft thing is really a weekend thing with the kids. I wouldn't call myself a crafter, exactly. It's more like, we have papercraft Saturday maybe once a month. We sit at the kitchen table, print something off, and cut and fold for an hour or two. Then it goes on a shelf and the kids show it to their grandma when she visits.

**Interviewer:** How did you get into it?

**Sarah:** Honestly, by accident. My older one — he was maybe six at the time — he was on the couch with the iPad and he found this craft blog with a free polyhedral dragon PDF. He wanted to make it. I printed it out, we did it together that weekend, and it actually turned out kind of cool? It's still on his shelf. After that it became a thing we do.

**Interviewer:** How often does it happen now, would you say?

**Sarah:** Roughly monthly. Sometimes we'll skip a month if there's a soccer tournament or whatever. Maybe ten or twelve times a year. It's never an all-day thing — if it goes past two hours, I've lost the kids. The seven-year-old especially, she'll just wander off and the project dies.

## Section 2 — Most recent project, end to end

**Interviewer:** Tell me about the last papercraft thing you built. What was it?

**Sarah:** The most recent one was a low-poly bunny. Easter-ish, even though we did it the weekend after. My daughter picked it from Pinterest — she scrolls Pinterest with me sometimes when I'm looking for dinner ideas. She saw the bunny and said she wanted it.

**Interviewer:** Walk me through it from "I want to make this" to "it's on the shelf."

**Sarah:** OK so — Saturday morning, after breakfast. I tap the pin, it goes to some craft site, there's a free PDF download. I download it, plug in the printer. The PDF is, I think, four pages? I print on regular paper because I'd already learned the hard way that my inkjet jams on heavy cardstock. So we print regular, then I tape the pages onto cardstock with a glue stick — actually I rub a glue stick on the back and press it onto a cardstock sheet, and then we cut out the shapes. That part the kids actually love, the cutting.

**Interviewer:** When you say you'd learned the hard way — what happened there?

**Sarah:** Oh, the dragon, the original one. I tried to print directly on cardstock and the printer ate two sheets. I had to open the back, pull the paper out, and the next sheet came out smeared. So I just don't do that anymore. Regular paper, glue to cardstock, cut.

**Interviewer:** OK. Back to the bunny — what happened next?

**Sarah:** So we cut everything out, and there's like… I don't know, twenty pieces? Some are little triangles. The seven-year-old does the bigger pieces, I do the small ones. Then we fold along the dotted lines. The PDF had tab numbers — like, tab 1 matches to tab 1 — but the font was tiny. My daughter kept asking me "which number is this?" and squinting. She thought a 6 was an 8 at one point. Then we glue. We use a glue stick mostly, sometimes a tiny dot of school glue for the tricky tabs.

**Interviewer:** How did it end up?

**Sarah:** Honestly, fine. The bunny is on the shelf. It's a little lopsided — one ear is kind of folded over because we lost a piece. Like, literally lost. It fell on the floor and the dog probably ate it. We just powered through and it looks a little wonky but the kids are proud of it.

**Interviewer:** What part took longest?

**Sarah:** Finding it. Honestly. Finding the right model was longer than building it. I probably scrolled Pinterest for twenty minutes looking at things and going, "no, that's too hard," "no, that's ugly," "no, that one doesn't say it's free." The actual building was maybe ninety minutes.

**Interviewer:** What part frustrated you most?

**Sarah:** The tiny numbers on the tabs. And losing the piece. When the piece fell on the floor my daughter was about to cry. She thought she'd ruined it.

**Interviewer:** What part was actually fun?

**Sarah:** Cutting. The kids love scissors. And the moment where you fold the last face and it pops into a 3D shape — that's the payoff. Both kids go "oooh." That's the whole reason we do it.

## Section 3 — Tools and workflow

**Interviewer:** What software do you use as part of papercraft, if any?

**Sarah:** Um… none, really. I use the PDF that someone else made. I print it. That's it.

**Interviewer:** Has there ever been a time you wanted to make something custom?

**Sarah:** Yeah, once. My son had this 3D printed dinosaur model — like a little STL file, I think? — and he wanted a paper version of it. I went down a rabbit hole. I think the program was called Pepakura? Someone on a blog said that's what you use.

**Interviewer:** How did that go?

**Sarah:** Bad. I gave up. I opened it and there were like — windows everywhere, and a 3D view, and buttons I didn't recognize, and I clicked something and the model fell apart on screen into a hundred pieces. I spent twenty-five minutes trying to figure out what any of it meant and I just closed it. We made something else that day.

**Interviewer:** Did you ever go back?

**Sarah:** No. Never. I think about it sometimes, like when my son asks for something specific. But it's not worth twenty-five minutes of me being frustrated in front of the kids. I just find the nearest thing on Pinterest.

**Interviewer:** If you had to sketch your workflow on a whiteboard, what would the boxes be?

**Sarah:** Boxes? Um. Box one — find a model on Pinterest. Box two — print on regular paper. Box three — glue to cardstock. Box four — cut. Box five — fold. Box six — glue tabs. Box seven — put on shelf. That's it. Seven boxes.

## Section 4 — Pain probes

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time a model came out wrong.

**Sarah:** Oh god. OK, there was this fox. A few months ago. We cut everything out, and when we started gluing — one of the side panels just didn't line up. Like, the tab was way too short for the edge it was supposed to glue to. I redid the cut, thinking I'd messed it up. Same result. So either I printed it wrong, or the model was wrong, and I genuinely could not tell. My daughter was getting bored and I just shoved the tab in there with extra glue and it's kind of bulging on one side. It's on the shelf with the seam showing.

**Interviewer:** When you print and start cutting, are you ever surprised by what's on it?

**Sarah:** Sometimes, yeah. Like — is this scissors or do I need the Xacto? Is this regular paper or do I need cardstock? Some models say, some don't. The bunny didn't say. I had to just guess based on how thick the dragon had felt.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever wished a piece were split differently?

**Sarah:** Hmm. I haven't thought about it that way. The pieces are what they are, I guess. But… you know what, yes, actually — the dragon had these two little wing pieces that were so small I had to do them with tweezers. I kind of wished those were just part of the body, even if it meant a less detailed wing. The detail isn't worth it when my seven-year-old can't help.

**Interviewer:** How do you handle the tabs and glue flaps?

**Sarah:** Glue stick, mostly. Tape sometimes if it's a flat connection. The seven-year-old uses tape, I use glue stick. We try to keep the tape inside.

**Interviewer:** After it's assembled, what does it usually look like — clean, good enough, seams everywhere?

**Sarah:** "Good enough." Definitely. I can see all the seams if I look. The kids don't care. Grandma doesn't care. It's good enough.

## Section 5 — Edit and undo behaviors

**Interviewer:** Once a model is unfolded into the PDF, do you ever change anything before printing?

**Sarah:** No. I literally just hit print. I wouldn't know how to change it.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever used an "undo" feature in a papercraft tool?

**Sarah:** I mean, the only tool I ever opened was Pepakura that one time, and I don't remember if I undid anything. I think I just closed it without saving. Sorry, I'm not very useful on this one.

**Interviewer:** No, that's helpful. Do you save your in-progress unfolds?

**Sarah:** I just save the PDF in a Downloads folder somewhere and probably never find it again. If we redo a model — which we don't, really — I'd re-download it from Pinterest.

## Section 6 — Assembly help

**Interviewer:** When you're assembling, how do you keep track of which piece goes where?

**Sarah:** The numbers. Tab 1 to tab 1. When the numbers are big enough. The bunny had tiny ones and we kept guessing.

**Interviewer:** Do you ever lose your place?

**Sarah:** All the time. Especially if I take a break to go deal with the kids. I come back and I'm like, where was I. Usually I just hold the half-done thing up and turn it around until I see where the next tab needs to go.

**Interviewer:** Do you use anything besides the printed sheet?

**Sarah:** The photo on the Pinterest pin. I keep my phone on the table with the picture of what it's supposed to look like. That's actually how we figured out the bunny's ears were supposed to point up, not back.

## Section 7 — Magic wand

**Interviewer:** If a software fairy could give you any one feature for your next project, what would it be?

**Sarah:** A slider. A difficulty slider. Like — I want to make this bunny, but I want the seven-year-old version of it. Slide it to the left and the tiny triangles fuse together, the tab numbers print huge, and the piece count goes down. Like, "simplify this thing until my seven-year-old can do most of it." That's the feature. That's all I want.

**Interviewer:** Silliest most over-the-top thing?

**Sarah:** [laughs] OK, this is ridiculous, but — a button that says "print this on paper I actually have." Like, it looks at my printer and goes, this is regular paper, OK, I'll adjust the model to be sturdy in regular paper instead of demanding cardstock. Or it just prints it onto whatever's in the tray and makes it work. That would be amazing.

**Interviewer:** If unfolding software disappeared entirely and the model just magically appeared as a perfect PDF, would you miss anything?

**Sarah:** No. [laughs] I would not miss anything. I want the PDF. I don't want to be in the software. The software, for me, is the wall.

## Section 8 — Cool-down

**Interviewer:** Anything I should have asked that I didn't?

**Sarah:** Maybe — who am I doing this for. Because I'm not doing it for me. I'm doing it for the kids. So anything that makes them able to participate more is the win. The bunny, the dragon — those aren't about me getting a perfect bunny. They're about my daughter cutting a shape and going "I made that." If the design needs her to use tweezers and read 6-point font, she didn't make it, I made it. And then it didn't count.

**Interviewer:** Anything else you want in the notes?

**Sarah:** Just — finding the right model is at least half the problem. Like, before I ever open a PDF, the question is "is this going to work for a Saturday with two kids?" And there's no way to know until I've already printed it. I'd love to know that earlier. That's it. That's my whole pitch.

**Interviewer:** Thank you, Sarah. This was really helpful.

**Sarah:** Yeah, of course. Tell my kids you made me feel important.
