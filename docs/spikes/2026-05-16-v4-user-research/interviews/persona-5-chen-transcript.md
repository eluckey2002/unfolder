# Interview transcript — Mr. Chen
Date: 2026-05-16 (synthetic)
Interviewer: UX researcher (neutral, evidence-seeking)
Interviewee: Mr. Chen, 52, middle-school math teacher running an annual polyhedra unit

## Section 1 — Warmup

**Interviewer:** Thanks for making time. To start — tell me a bit about yourself. What you do, where you live, the kind of stuff you make.

**Mr. Chen:** Sure. I teach seventh-grade math in a public middle school in California — twenty-two years now, same district. Five sections a day, twenty-eight kids per section, give or take. The thing I'm guessing you want to hear about is the polyhedra unit. It lands in week fourteen, right after winter break, and the centerpiece is three days where every kid builds a paper model of a named solid. I print the nets, they cut, score, fold, glue. That's the "stuff I make" — or really, that I help one hundred and forty twelve-year-olds make.

**Interviewer:** How did you get into building paper models?

**Mr. Chen:** Honestly, it was the unit that got me into it, not the other way around. When I started teaching, the textbook had a sad little half-page on Platonic solids and the kids never really got it. About year three I tried handing out cardstock nets — cube, tetrahedron — and the unit transformed. Kids who never spoke up in class were suddenly the experts at folding. So I leaned in. I started collecting nets, then making my own, then trying the more exotic ones — truncated forms, stellated forms. The papercraft is downstream of the teaching.

**Interviewer:** How often are you actually building, then?

**Mr. Chen:** Heavily seasonal. Three intense days in February, plus maybe two weekends in January when I'm prototyping new nets. Outside of that, almost nothing. I'm not the hobbyist who's building a paper dragon on a Tuesday night.

## Section 2 — Most recent project, end to end

**Interviewer:** Tell me about the last papercraft thing you built. What was it?

**Mr. Chen:** Last February, the truncated icosahedron. The soccer ball. I wanted to add it to the unit because it's the bridge between the Platonic solids and real-world objects — kids recognize it instantly, and then I can show them it's twelve pentagons and twenty hexagons and suddenly geometry is on the playground. I had it on my list for two years. Last year I finally pulled the trigger.

**Interviewer:** Walk me through it from "I want this" to "it's on the shelf."

**Mr. Chen:** Okay. Step one was: does my curriculum vendor have it? I use Twinkl mostly, sometimes TeachersPayTeachers. Twinkl had the five Platonics and three of the Archimedeans, but no truncated icosahedron. So step two: find it elsewhere. I spent a Saturday afternoon — I want to say three or four hours — searching for a printable net. I found one PDF on a hobbyist site that looked promising, printed one, assembled it on my kitchen table. The pentagons were not regular. They were close. But when you assemble it, the asymmetry compounds at every seam. The finished thing had a sort of lopsided look, and one of the hexagons wouldn't close — there was a visible gap maybe two millimeters wide.

**Interviewer:** What did you do at that point?

**Mr. Chen:** I tried two more sources. One was a generator — you pick a solid from a dropdown and it gives you a net. Same problem, different flavor. The pentagons came out as irregular pentagons that were *almost* regular. I'm a math teacher. I can measure with calipers. A twelve-year-old can't measure with calipers, but a twelve-year-old can absolutely see when a soccer ball isn't a soccer ball. That's worse than not building it.

**Interviewer:** Did you finish it?

**Mr. Chen:** I built one in front of myself, in my kitchen, and I judged it wasn't classroom-ready. So I cut the truncated icosahedron from last year's unit and went with the cuboctahedron instead, which Twinkl did have. I lost the lesson I wanted. The kids got a fine model — but I lost the soccer-ball moment.

**Interviewer:** What part of the process took longest?

**Mr. Chen:** Hunting for a usable source. Three or four hours that Saturday, plus another two the following weekend after the first one failed. The actual assembly was twenty minutes. The cutting and printing for the kids — once I have the PDF, that's a fixed cost: one hundred and forty sheets, two sections of my printer's tray, takes a prep period.

**Interviewer:** What part frustrated you most?

**Mr. Chen:** That the failure was invisible at the PDF stage. The pentagons *looked* fine on screen. I had to print one, cut one, glue one before I could tell the math was off. There's no preview that shows me whether I'm about to waste an afternoon.

**Interviewer:** What part was fun?

**Mr. Chen:** When I built the cuboctahedron prototype — the backup model — it went together like clockwork and I remembered why I love this unit. Twenty minutes of folding and you're holding a shape that humans have been holding for two thousand years. That doesn't get old.

## Section 3 — Tools and workflow

**Interviewer:** What software, if any, do you use as part of papercraft?

**Mr. Chen:** Almost none, honestly. I use Acrobat to print. I use my curriculum vendor's website to download PDFs. Last summer I tried to use a free online net generator — I don't remember the name, it was open-source, the interface was Russian I think — to make a stellated dodecahedron net. I got something out of it but I couldn't tell if it was right, and the labels were unreadable.

**Interviewer:** Tell me more about that — when you first tried it, how did it go?

**Mr. Chen:** Frustrating. I'm comfortable with math but not with software. The tool wanted me to upload a mesh file. I don't know what a mesh file is in practice — I know what it is conceptually. I had to look up where to even get one for a stellated dodecahedron. Eventually I found a file on a 3D-printing site, fed it in, got a net back. But the labels — it numbered the edges but the font was tiny, and when I printed it, half the numbers were illegible. For my use case, that's a dealbreaker. Kids match edge seven to edge seven. If a kid can't read "seven," I have twenty-eight hands going up.

**Interviewer:** Was there a tool you wanted to use but bounced off?

**Mr. Chen:** Pepakura, I've heard of. I looked at it maybe five years ago. I read on a forum that the free version doesn't let you save, and the paid version was a one-time license but only ran on Windows, and our school laptops are Chromebooks. I never installed it. There's also — what's the 3D one — Blender. Friends have told me Blender can do this. I opened Blender once, saw the interface, closed it. That's not a tool for me.

**Interviewer:** If you sketched your workflow on a whiteboard, what would the boxes be?

**Mr. Chen:** Box one: pick the solid I want to teach. Box two: check Twinkl. If it's there, jump to box six. Box three, if it's not: search the web for an hour. Box four: download something, print one, build one, evaluate it. Box five: either adopt it or throw it out and pick a different solid. Box six: print one hundred and forty sheets. Box seven: paper-cutter to trim to size if needed. Box eight: distribute to kids. The whole left-hand side of the whiteboard is "find a trustworthy net," and that's where I spend ninety percent of the unpredictable time.

## Section 4 — Pain probes

**Interviewer:** Tell me about a time a model came out wrong.

**Mr. Chen:** The truncated icosahedron I just described. And there's an older one — three years ago, an icosahedron from a free PDF where the tabs were on the wrong side of half the edges. Kids tried to glue and ended up with the printed face on the inside. I caught it after section one of five, pulled the rest of the prints, hand-redrew the tab orientation on a master copy, reprinted during lunch. That was a rough day.

**Interviewer:** When you print a sheet and start cutting, are you ever surprised by what's on it?

**Mr. Chen:** Yes. Scale, mostly. I'll get a PDF that opens fine on screen, and when I print, it's been scaled to A4 or it's tiled across two pages because the source was European. I once had a net that looked great in preview and printed across two sheets because the bounding box was a centimeter too wide for US Letter. I didn't catch it until I'd printed twenty-eight copies. Twenty-eight wasted sheets — that's not the end of the world, but it's a tell that the tool wasn't built for my paper size.

**Interviewer:** Have you wished a piece were split differently than the tool gave you? Or merged?

**Mr. Chen:** Yes, both. The stellated dodecahedron I mentioned — the generator gave me one big connected net that didn't fit on a single sheet. I would much rather have had it split into two pieces with a clear seam, scaled to fit Letter. And the opposite problem on a simpler shape — sometimes a generator splits something into more pieces than needed, and now my kids are gluing three separate parts when one would do. More glue joints means more failure points means more frustrated kids.

**Interviewer:** How do you handle tabs and glue flaps?

**Mr. Chen:** I want the tabs included on the net and I want them clearly marked — a different shade, or a dashed line. The Twinkl nets do this well: tabs are a lighter gray. The free generators usually don't, and the kids glue the wrong side. The pre-folded score lines are nice but optional — I can have the kids score with a butter knife along a ruler.

**Interviewer:** After assembly, what does the finished result usually look like?

**Mr. Chen:** With Twinkl Platonics? Clean. They look like the textbook picture. With anything I sourced myself? Mixed. Visible seams, sometimes asymmetry, the occasional gap. Good enough for a kid's display shelf, not good enough for the front of the classroom.

## Section 5 — Edit and undo behaviors

**Interviewer:** Once a model is unfolded — by software or by a PDF — do you ever change anything before printing?

**Mr. Chen:** I rescale, occasionally. I enlarge the edge labels if the font is too small. I almost never edit the geometry itself. I wouldn't know how, and I don't trust myself to do it right. If the unfold is wrong, my move is to find a different unfold, not fix the one I have.

**Interviewer:** Have you ever "undone" something in a papercraft tool?

**Mr. Chen:** I don't think I've used a tool long enough to need undo. The generator I tried — I just regenerated. Pressed the button again. If I had to undo a specific change, I'm not sure I'd find the button.

**Interviewer:** Do you save in-progress unfolds, or start fresh?

**Mr. Chen:** Fresh, always. I save the final PDF. I don't save anything upstream of the PDF because I don't trust I'd be able to reopen it next year and get the same output. The PDF is the durable artifact.

## Section 6 — Assembly help

**Interviewer:** When kids assemble, how do they keep track of which piece goes where?

**Mr. Chen:** Edge labels. Numbered edges. Edge seven on face A glues to edge seven on face B. It's the single most important feature of a good net for my use case. Without it, the kids guess, and twenty-eight kids guessing means twenty-eight wrong models.

**Interviewer:** When a kid loses their place, what gets them back on track?

**Mr. Chen:** Me, walking over and pointing. Or — and this is something I started doing four years ago — I print a small reference image of the finished solid in the corner of the net itself. A thumbnail. So they can glance at it and orient. That helped a lot.

**Interviewer:** Do they use anything beyond the printed sheet — a 3D model, a video?

**Mr. Chen:** Some kids ask if they can look up the shape on their Chromebook. I let them — usually they find a rotating image on Wikipedia. Twelve-year-olds in 2026 are very comfortable with a 3D viewer; some of them are honestly better at spatial reasoning on screen than on paper.

## Section 7 — Magic wand

**Interviewer:** If a software fairy could give you any one feature for your next polyhedra unit, what would it be?

**Mr. Chen:** Give me any polyhedron — name it, or upload a definition — and get back a clean, printable, US-Letter-sized net with numbered edges in a font a twelve-year-old can read. Mathematically exact. Tabs included and clearly marked. A thumbnail of the assembled shape in the corner. One sheet per kid. That's the wand.

**Interviewer:** And the silly, over-the-top version?

**Mr. Chen:** Type "truncated icosahedron, half-meter diameter, soccer-ball coloring, classroom-grade" and have one hundred and forty sheets appear in my printer tray, pre-collated by section. And a little certificate of mathematical correctness — like a hash, or a signature — so I can show my principal the math has been verified, not approximated.

**Interviewer:** If the software disappeared and the perfect PDF just appeared, would you miss anything?

**Mr. Chen:** No. I have no romantic attachment to the tool. I am not a hobbyist. The PDF is what I want. The faster I get to a trustworthy PDF, the better.

## Section 8 — Cool-down

**Interviewer:** Anything I should have asked that I didn't?

**Mr. Chen:** Two things. One, you didn't ask about cost. My district pays for textbooks and consumables; subscription software is essentially impossible for me. A one-time license, free, or pay-per-PDF I can expense — those work. A monthly subscription does not. Two, privacy. Anything that asks me to upload student data, or even sometimes anything cloud-based, has to go through district privacy review, and that can take months. A tool that runs entirely in my browser, without an account, is much easier to adopt than one that wants me to sign up.

**Interviewer:** Anything you want in the notes?

**Mr. Chen:** That mathematical correctness is not negotiable for my use case. "Looks about right" is worse than nothing — because then I waste an afternoon and a stack of cardstock discovering it's wrong. If a tool tells me a shape is a truncated icosahedron, the pentagons need to be regular pentagons. That's the bar. Everything else is a bonus.

**Interviewer:** Thank you, Mr. Chen.

**Mr. Chen:** Of course. Good luck with the research.
